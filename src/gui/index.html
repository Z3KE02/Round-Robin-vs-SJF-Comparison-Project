Evaluation & Final Analysis

Final Conclusion & Analytical Comparison

⚖️ Fairness versus efficiency

Round Robin ensures high fairness by sharing CPU time equally through time slicing, preventing starvation. However, frequent context switching can reduce overall efficiency. SRTF maximizes efficiency by prioritizing short jobs to achieve the minimum average waiting time, but sacrifices fairness as long processes may suffer from starvation.

⏱️ Effect of time slicing versus shortest-job preference

Time slicing (RR) guarantees regular CPU allocation, making it ideal for interactive systems where no process waits too long to be scheduled. Shortest-job preference (SRTF) heavily optimizes for throughput by quickly finishing tasks, but a steady stream of short tasks can indefinitely delay execution of longer ones.

🚀 Effect on first response time

Round Robin generally provides a better and more predictable first response time for all processes since they all get a turn within a bounded time frame. SRTF provides lightning-fast response times for short jobs, but significantly poorer response times for long jobs if short jobs keep arriving.

↔️ Effect of quantum size on Round Robin behavior

A smaller quantum improves response time but increases context switching overhead, hurting overall throughput. A larger quantum reduces overhead but makes RR degrade into First-Come-First-Served (FCFS), which hurts interactive response times. The optimal quantum balances response time and context switch overhead.

⚡ Whether SRTF gives a strong advantage to short jobs

Yes, SRTF heavily favors short jobs by immediately preempting currently running longer jobs. This yields the mathematically optimal minimum average waiting time for the system, making it highly effective when the system has many quick tasks to complete, at the direct expense of long jobs.
